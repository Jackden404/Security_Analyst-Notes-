Formulating a hypothesis requires threat intelligence, but testing that hypothesis requires analytical techniques. These data analysis techniques allow hunters to sift through millions of log lines to extract anomalies.

---
## 🗂️ Stacking (Frequency Analysis)

Stacking is the most fundamental threat hunting technique. You group logs by a specific field (e.g., process name, User-Agent, registry key) and sort by the number of occurrences (frequency).

[!IMPORTANT] **Outliers and Rarity:** In stacking analysis, the primary indicator of suspicion is: **Values that appear very rarely**.

In standard networks, legitimate configurations and user habits occur millions of times, producing massive spikes in frequency. Attacker command lines, malware payloads, and script-based User-Agents are outliers, appearing only once or twice (least-frequency analysis).

---
## 📉 Long Tail Analysis

Long tail analysis is a statistical extension of stacking, looking exclusively at the far right of the probability distribution.


```
Frequency
  ▲
  │   [ Normal Activity ] (Explorer, Chrome, svchost)
  │   █████████
  │   █████████
  │   █████████
  │   █████████
  │   █████████
  │   █████████   [ Anomalies / Attack Foots ]
  │   █████████   ██                                         ██
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────►
                                                              Values (Long Tail)
```

[!NOTE] **Focus of Long Tail Analysis:** Long Tail Analysis focuses on: **Rare occurrences that may indicate threats**.

Attackers attempting to blend in using legitimate built-in administrative binaries (e.g., `cmd.exe` or `powershell.exe`) generate highly unusual parent-child combinations (such as `sqlservr.exe` spawning `cmd.exe`), which will reside at the very tip of the long tail.

---
## 🕸️ Pivoting: Following the Thread

Finding a single indicator is never the end of a hunt. Responders must map the complete footprint of the intrusion.

[!TIP] **Pivoting Workflow:** In pivoting, what do you do after finding a suspicious indicator? **Follow the trail to related indicators across different data sources**.

For example:

1. Find a suspicious PowerShell execution on a workstation.

2. **Pivot to network logs:** See that PowerShell connected to IP `185.220.101.42`.

3. **Pivot to DNS logs:** Discover which other workstations resolved the domain mapped to that IP.

4. **Pivot to endpoint EDR logs:** Look for files written or credentials modified on those newly discovered systems.
