In digital forensics, the administrative procedures used to handle, secure, and track evidence are just as important as the technical investigation. A failure to document ownership or protect data integrity can render critical evidence legally worthless.

---
## ⛓️ The Importance of Chain of Custody (CoC)

A **Chain of Custody (CoC) form** is a chronological paper trail that documents the custody, transfer, analysis, and storage of physical and digital evidence.

[!CAUTION] **Admissibility Risk:** If you cannot show an unbroken custody log: **The evidence may be inadmissible in court**. Opposing counsel will argue that the evidence was exposed to unauthorized access, tampered with, or modified, resulting in the evidence being thrown out of legal proceedings.

---
## ⏳ Volatility & Order of Acquisition (RFC 3227)

Not all digital data persists equally. Volatile data resides in active system memory and disappears as soon as power is cut or the system reboots.

[!IMPORTANT] **Order of Volatility:** Based on standard acquisition guidelines (RFC 3227), you must prioritize: **Volatile evidence like RAM and running processes** before acquiring non-volatile data (such as hard drive contents or archival tapes).

### RFC 3227 Order of Volatility:

1. **CPU Registers & Cache:** Nanoseconds.
2. **Routing Tables, ARP Cache, Process Table:** Seconds to minutes.
3. **Physical Memory (RAM):** Lost immediately on power cut.
4. **Temporary File Systems:** Lost on system reboot.
5. **Non-Volatile Disk Space (HDD/SSD):** Persistent, but subject to background write actions.
6. **Remote Logs & Backups:** Persistent, subject to remote log rotation limits.

## 🔢 Cryptographic Hashing: The Digital Seal

Hashing is the mathematical mechanism used to verify that digital evidence has not been altered since collection.

```
[ Evidence Source File / Image ] ──► [ SHA-256 Hash Function ] ──► [ Fixed Fingerprint / Hash Value ]
```

[!TIP] **Verification of Integrity:** The primary purpose of hashing in forensics is: **To verify the evidence has not been altered**.

By running a hashing algorithm (such as SHA-256 or MD5) on the original drive and comparing it to the hash of the forensic clone, investigators provide mathematical proof of a bit-perfect copy. Hashes are recorded in the Chain of Custody document and recalculations are performed at every stage of the lifecycle.
