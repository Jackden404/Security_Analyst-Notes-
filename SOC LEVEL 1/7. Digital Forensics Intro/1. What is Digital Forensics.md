Digital Forensics is the forensic science of identifying, preserving, recovering, analyzing, and presenting facts about digital evidence. It spans both technical investigation and legal compliance to ensure evidence stands up under rigorous cross-examination.

---
## 🔑 The Golden Rule of Digital Forensics

When handling a security incident or a legal investigation, the investigator must follow the cardinal guideline of evidence preservation:

[!IMPORTANT] **The Golden Rule:** In any investigation, the absolute rule of forensics is to: **Preserve the original evidence at all costs**. You should never perform analysis directly on the original drive or media. Instead, you create a bit-stream forensic copy (clone) of the evidence, verify its integrity via hash calculation, and perform your investigation exclusively on the copy.

---
## 🗂️ Specialized Branches of Digital Forensics

Digital forensics is divided into several technical sub-disciplines:

- **Disk Forensics:** Analyzing hard drives, SSDs, and storage media to recover deleted files, inspect directory structures, and extract slack space data.

- **Network Forensics:** Inspecting raw packet captures (PCAPs), proxy logs, and firewall event streams to reconstruct attacker communication paths.

- **Cloud Forensics:** Investigating resources inside cloud infrastructures (AWS CloudTrail logs, Azure Active Directory records, API access events).

- **Mobile Forensics:** Extracting messages, app storage, and system settings from mobile OS systems.

- **Memory Forensics:**

[!NOTE] **RAM Analysis:** **Memory forensics** is the specific branch that analyzes RAM dumps. This reveals running processes, dynamic network connections, decrypted encryption keys, and loaded drivers that do not exist on disk and vanish once power is lost.

## ⚖️ Legal Frameworks & The Daubert Standard

In legal proceedings, digital evidence must prove its authenticity and integrity. Hashing (using MD5, SHA-1, or SHA-256) guarantees that the copy matches the original exactly.

[!WARNING] **Admissibility in Court:** In the US legal system, the **Daubert standard** is used for: **Determining the admissibility of expert testimony and scientific evidence**.

Judges act as gatekeepers to evaluate:

1. Whether the technique has been tested.
2. Whether it has been peer-reviewed.
3. The known error rate.
4. Whether the tools and methods are generally accepted in the scientific community.
