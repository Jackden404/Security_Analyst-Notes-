An incident timeline is the single most important artifact in any investigation. It functions as the narrative backbone that transforms scattered log entries, forensic outputs, and analyst actions into a coherent, chronological story. When stakeholders ask "what happened," when legal needs to establish causation, or when regulators evaluate response times, they all start with the timeline.

---
## 🏗️ Anatomy of a Timeline Entry

A bad timeline is a list of relative timestamps with vague descriptions (e.g., _"about 30 minutes later, host looked clean"_). A professional timeline is precise and structured.

[!IMPORTANT] **Timeline Entry Components:** A proper incident timeline entry consists of four essential components: **Timestamp, Source, Event, Actor**.

- **Timestamp:** Precision in ISO 8601 UTC format.

- **Source:** Where the data came from (e.g., _Firewall, EDR, DNS, User report_).

- **Event:** What specific action took place (e.g., _Outbound HTTPS connection to updates-cdn.com_).

- **Actor:** Who or what process initiated the action (e.g., _PID 4212, svchost.exe_).

```
2026-03-15T14:23:07Z | Source: Firewall | Event: HTTPS connection to updates-cdn[.]com | Actor: svchost.exe (PID 4212)
```

---
## 🕒 Timestamp Normalization

In a real incident, you will collect logs from multiple systems located in different time zones (e.g., EDR in UTC, firewall in local office time, domain controller in corporate headquarters time).

[!WARNING] **The Importance of UTC:** All timestamps must be normalized to UTC before merging because **different sources may use different timezones, causing events to appear out of order**. Failing to normalize timestamps can result in your timeline showing payloads being downloaded _before_ the phishing email was opened, destroying the credibility of your report.

## 🛠️ Specialized Timeline Tools

Security teams use specialized tools to parse diverse logs and assemble them into a master timeline.

|Tool|Type|Key Advantage|
|---|---|---|
|**Plaso** _(log2timeline)_|Forensic Engine|**It ingests hundreds of formats and normalizes timestamps into a super-timeline**. It automates log collection across diverse operating systems and applications.|
|**Timesketch**|Collaborative Analysis|Allows multiple analysts to collaborate on, annotate, and search super-timelines in real time.|
|**SIEM Timeline Views**|Threat Monitoring|Provides immediate visual grouping of events within search dashboards (Splunk, Chronicle, Elastic).|

## 📝 Example: Real-World Incident Timeline

Here is an example of a normalized timeline documenting a ransomware incident:

- **2026-01-22T08:14:33Z** - _Email Gateway_ | Received phishing email delivered to `finance@company.com` with attachment `Invoice_2026.xlsm`.

- **2026-01-22T08:17:45Z** - _EDR Telemetry_ | Excel macro executed. Process `excel.exe` spawned `cmd.exe`.

- **2026-01-22T08:17:52Z** - _Windows Event Logs_ | `cmd.exe` executed PowerShell `Invoke-WebRequest` downloading malware payload `updates.exe`.

- **2026-01-22T08:18:03Z** - _Sysmon Logs_ | Process `updates.exe` created scheduled task `MicrosoftEdgeUpdate` for persistence.

- **2026-01-22T08:31:17Z** - _DNS logs_ | Outbound C2 beacons established to domain `staging-c2.com`.

- **2026-01-22T09:45:22Z** - _AD Security Logs_ | Lateral movement via SMB to database server using harvested administrator credentials.

- **2026-01-22T10:12:08Z** - _Proxy Logs_ | Data exfiltration using `rclone` to cloud storage platform.

- **2026-01-22T11:30:00Z** - _EDR Telemetry_ | Ransomware payload executed across network endpoints.
