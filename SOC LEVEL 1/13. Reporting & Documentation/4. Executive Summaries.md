The executive summary is the most critical and frequently read section of any incident report. The primary mistake analysts make is writing it with too much technical jargon. A CEO, board member, or general counsel does not need to know the specific Windows Event IDs or process registry keys modified. They need to know the high-level impact on operations, finance, and security posture.

---
## 🏗️ The SOHO Framework

The SOHO framework is a structured method for drafting professional, concise executive summaries:

|Phase|Meaning|Focus Area|
|---|---|---|
|**S**|**Situation**|What happened, described in high-level, non-technical language.|
|**O**|**Objective**|What was compromised or at risk. Quantifies the scope and impact.|
|**H**|**Handling**|What actions the security team took to contain and resolve the threat.|
|**O**|**Outlook**|What the current state of security is and the next steps for prevention.|

[!IMPORTANT] **SOHO Framework Origin:** In the SOHO framework, the "S" stands for **Situation**. It establishes the baseline facts of the incident (e.g., _when did it occur, what was the primary threat_).

---
## 📈 Quantifying Business Impact

Security reports must speak the language of business leaders rather than that of technicians.

[!WARNING] **Speaking to Leadership:** Executive summaries must quantify impact in business terms because **leadership makes decisions based on financial and operational impact, not technical specifics**.

- _Technical language:_ "Malware encrypted files in folder shares and terminated services."

- _Business language:_ "The attack disrupted business operations for approximately 26 hours, affecting 47 workstations and 2 file servers."

---
## 📏 Length and Formatting Tips

[!TIP] **Keep It Concise:** The recommended maximum length for an executive summary is **one paragraph, under 200 words**. Leaders skim reports under high-pressure conditions. If your executive summary is three pages long, key messages will be lost. Give them the essentials:

1. **Lead with the Date & Root Cause:** Do not bury the main facts.

2. **Quantify Everything:** Use exact numbers (e.g., _"8,200 records"_ instead of _"many records"_).

3. **End with a Posture Outlook:** Reassure stakeholders by defining what remediation plans are already underway.
