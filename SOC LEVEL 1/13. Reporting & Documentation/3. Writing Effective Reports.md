An incident report is the definitive record of a security breach. It is the document read by executives, legal teams, external regulators, and IT support crews. Poorly structured or overly technical reporting can obscure critical facts, leading to slower remediation and increased business risk.

---
## 🗂️ Report Structure

A professional incident report is divided into clear sections targeted at different stakeholders:

```
[ Executive Summary ] (Non-Technical Leadership)
         │
         ▼
[ Incident Overview & Timeline ] (Incident Responders & Managers)
         │
         ▼
[ Technical Analysis & Impact Assessment ] (Security Engineering & Forensics)
         │
         ▼
[ Recommendations & Action Plan ] (Security & IT Teams)
```

[!IMPORTANT] **Report Order:** The **Executive Summary** should come first in an incident report **because it is the only section most leadership will read**. Executives do not have time to read through hundreds of lines of command line logs. They need a high-level summary (100–200 words) defining: _What happened_, _What was the impact_, _What did the team do_, and _Are we secure now_.

---
## 🎯 Writing SMART Recommendations

Vague recommendations (e.g., _"conduct more training"_ or _"improve security controls"_) fail to guide the IT team toward actual remediation. Recommendations must be Specific, Measurable, Actionable, Relevant, and Time-bound (SMART).

[!TIP] **A Well-Written Recommendation:** An example of a well-written recommendation is: **"Deploy CrowdStrike Falcon to all Windows endpoints by Q2 2024"**. This clearly defines the _what_ (CrowdStrike Falcon), the _where_ (all Windows endpoints), and the _when_ (by Q2 2024), providing a measurable benchmark for success.

---
## ⚖️ Objective Reporting (Blameless Culture)

When a breach occurs because an employee clicked on a link or opened an attachment, reports must remain objective.

[!WARNING] **Avoiding Blame:** Incident reports should avoid blaming users for phishing clicks because **the focus should be on control gaps that allowed the compromise regardless of user action**. Human error is inevitable. A secure system relies on defense-in-depth (e.g., email filters, EDR policies, and network segmentation) to mitigate human slips. Focus on _why the macro was allowed to execute_ or _why the credential was allowed to be used from an untrusted IP_, rather than pointing fingers at individual employees.

---
## 📝 Comparison: Writing Style

### ❌ Vague (Before)

On January 22, a phishing email was received by a user in the finance department. The user opened the attachment which contained malware. The SOC team detected the malware and isolated the infected computer.

### ✅ Professional (After)

On January 22, a phishing email targeting the finance department delivered a macro-enabled Excel document containing a ransomware payload. Initial access was achieved at 08:17 UTC when the recipient enabled macros. The attacker established persistence, moved laterally to the file server, exfiltrated 12GB of financial records via MEGA, and deployed ransomware across 47 workstations at 11:30 UTC. Containment began at 11:31 UTC. All affected systems were restored from backups by January 24. Root cause was the absence of macro execution controls and lack of network segmentation.
